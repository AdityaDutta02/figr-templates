Research · 01 02.07
The average page visit. How much gets read.

Nielsen Norman Group tracked 232 users across thousands of pages. They scan in an F-shape. Two horizontal passes at the top. Then a vertical skim down the left. Not because they're lazy — because time is limited and content is everywhere.

Rule · 01 03.07
The first two words of every line are critical.

The third word is read far less often. Write headings so the most informative word comes first. Not: "This is a guide to improving conversion." But: "Conversion guide: 5 things that move the number."

Rule · 02 04.07
Statistics and bold text create scanning anchors.

"7 in 10 users abandon at this point" — the eye stops. A single bolded phrase in a paragraph becomes a natural landing point. Subheadings create a layer-cake pattern — the best scanning pattern your users can follow.

Rule · 03 05.07
Short paragraphs give the eye a natural resting point.

Long paragraphs get skimmed from the first line and dropped. Two to three lines, then break. This creates white space, resting points, and a visual rhythm that invites reading instead of triggering the skip reflex.

Rule · 04 06.07
Most of your content lives where eyes never land.

Captions under images. Content after the third scroll. The middle of a sentence. Your most important point buried mid-paragraph. If it's not in the first word of a heading or the first line of a paragraph — it might as well not exist.
